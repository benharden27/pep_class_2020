    Temperature, Salinity and Density

    Physical Oceanography is the study of how water moves. This includes ocean currents, waves and large scale ocean circulation. Some of the most fundimental ideas revolve around the key physical properties of the water: Temperature, Salinity and Density. We will explore these properties and how they allow or limit movement of water in the ocean.

    1.1.1 How we measure Temperature and Salinity

    The basic tool for measuring the temperature and salinity of the ocean is the CTD (Conductivity-Temperature-Depth) device which we lower from the side of a ship to record a vertical trace (or profile) of the ocean.

    1.3.1 Exercise 1: Surface temperature and salinity

    Except for a few specific environments (such as deep-sea vents), temperature and salinity are changed solely in the surface ocean.

    Temperature is changed via:

    • Heating from the sun’s shortwave radiation
    • Cooling from the ocean’s longwave radiation
    • Cooling from a wind colder than the surface

    Salinity is changed via:

    • Freshening from rain or river input
    • Increasing salinity due to evaporation - the salt is left behind when the water is evaporated.
